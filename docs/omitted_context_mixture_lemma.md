# Omitted-Context Mixture Lemma Proof Draft

Issue: #4, "Proof: Formalize omitted-context mixture lemma"

Status: revised proof-review draft; proof-critic disposition `REVISE`, pending
PI decisions on A-009 and A-010 before acceptance or manuscript use.

## 1. Why This Lemma Matters

The project asks why a cognitive model can look empirically superior even when
its extra psychological machinery is not part of the data-generating process.
The first step is modest but load-bearing: once a contextual cause of a latent
decision parameter is omitted, the analyst no longer sees the original
individual-level choice law. The analyst sees an average of that law over the
latent parameter values that remain possible among observations with the same
recorded trial features and covariates.

That average is a mixture. This lemma proves the per-trial mixture identity. It
does not yet prove that a false complex model wins, and it does not claim that
omission is always harmful. Its role is to identify the population marginal
response law relevant to later trial-wise comparison arguments. Gaussian
heterogeneity, KL dominance, and finite-sample selection results may build on
this point only after their own target law and criterion conditions are stated.

In the grand scheme of the OCECM project, the lemma is the bridge from
"context affects latent cognition" to "a context-omitting analyst faces a
different per-trial statistical target." It explains how environmental or
socioeconomic context can become invisible in the model formula but still
remain present in the response surface being fitted. For participant-level
predictive or evidence targets, the appropriate object is a separately derived
joint mixture; this lemma does not supply that derivation.

## 2. Objects & Notation

Let `i` index participants and `t` index trials. For one generic
participant-trial observation, define:

- `Y` as a binary or multinomial choice outcome with support `Y_space`.
- `X` as the observed trial design vector.
- `Z` as observed participant covariates or observed context.
- `C` as omitted context.
- `U` as idiosyncratic latent heterogeneity.
- `Theta = h(Z, C, U)` as the latent decision parameter.
- `eta_star` as fixed parameters of the conditional choice kernel.

The analyst observes `(Y, X, Z)` and omits `C`. Because `C` and `U` are not
observed, the analyst generally does not know the participant's exact
`Theta`. Instead, among observations with the same recorded `(X = x, Z = z)`,
there is a conditional distribution of possible latent parameters:

```math
F_{\Theta | X,Z}(\theta | x,z).
```

The primary omitted-context marginal response law is therefore

```math
p_0(y | x,z) =
\int p(y | x,\theta; \eta^*) dF_{\Theta | X,Z}(\theta | x,z).
```

If `Theta` is discrete, the integral is a weighted sum over possible values of
`\theta`. If `Theta` is continuous, the integral is the corresponding
probability-weighted average. Either way, the meaning is the same: the analyst
averages the conditional choice probability over the latent parameter values
left unresolved after omitting context.

A simplified display with `F_{\Theta | Z}` is not a primary theorem assumption.
It is available only as a corollary under a fixed-design interpretation or an
explicit trial-design exogeneity condition.

## 3. Assumptions Used

1. A-001, Observed choice law: observed choices `Y_it` are generated by a
   latent decision parameter `theta_i` and observed trial features `X_it`
   through `p(Y_it | X_it, theta_i; eta)`.
2. A-009, Conditional choice-kernel sufficiency: for every `y` in `Y_space`,
   `E[\mathbf{1}\{Y = y\} \mid X,Z,\Theta] = p(y \mid X,\Theta; \eta^*)` almost surely. This is
   the minimal conditional-kernel equality used by the proof; it makes no
   claim that a participant-level joint response law factorizes.
3. A-010, Kernel measurability: for every `y` in `Y_space`, the map
   `(x,theta) -> p(y | x,theta; eta*)` is jointly Borel measurable. As a
   choice probability it is bounded in `[0,1]`, so integrability is automatic.
4. Regularity of conditioning: `Theta` takes values in a Borel (e.g. Polish)
   space, so that a regular conditional distribution `F_{\Theta | X,Z}(\cdot | x,z)`
   of `Theta` given `(X,Z)` exists and is a bona fide probability measure for
   `(X,Z)`-almost every `(x,z)`. In the project's binary/multinomial setting
   `Theta` is finite-dimensional and real-valued, so this holds automatically
   (Kallenberg, *Foundations of Modern Probability*, 2nd ed., Thm 6.3; Durrett,
   *Probability: Theory and Examples*, 5th ed., section 4.1.3).
5. A-002, Omitted context shifts latent parameter (interpretive only): the
   omitted contextual factor `C_i` affects `theta_i` through `h(Z_i, C_i, U_i)`.
   It explains when the mixture is context-induced but is not required for
   Lemma 1.1's probability identity.

Rejected assumption A-006 is not used in the primary theorem statement.
A-009 and A-010 are proposed decision-gated assumptions. Following PI
direction, the theorem package keeps `F_{\Theta | X,Z}` as the primary
statement and mentions the simplified `F_{\Theta | Z}` display only as a
corollary under additional conditions.

## 4. Formal Statement

### Lemma 1.1 (Omitted-Context Conditional Mixture)

Suppose the regular conditional distribution `F_{\Theta | X,Z}(\cdot | x,z)`
exists, A-009 holds, and the kernel is jointly measurable as in A-010. Then
for every `y` in the finite set `Y_space`,

```math
E[\mathbf{1}\{Y = y\} \mid X,Z,\Theta] = p(y \mid X,\Theta; \eta^*)
```

holds almost surely under the joint law of `(X,Z,Theta)`. Because `Y_space`
is finite, the equalities may be chosen with one common exceptional null set.
For `(X,Z)`-almost every `(x,z)`, the analyst who omits `C` observes

```math
P(Y = y | X = x, Z = z)
= \int p(y \mid x,\theta; \eta^*) dF_{\Theta | X,Z}(\theta | x,z).
```

### Corollary 1.2 (Fixed or Exogenous Trial Design)

If, in addition to Lemma 1.1, either `Theta` is conditionally independent of
`X` given `Z`, or assignment has the form `X = g(Z,V)` for measurable `g`
and a randomization device satisfying `V ⟂ (C,U) | Z`, then

```math
P(Y = y | X = x, Z = z)
= \int p(y \mid x,\theta; \eta^*) dF_{\Theta | Z}(\theta | z).
```

for `(X,Z)`-almost every `(x,z)`. A literal fixed conditional design is the
degenerate special case of the assignment condition. This corollary is not the
primary theorem statement.

## 5. Derivation

### 5.1 Strategy Overview

The proof is a conditional-expectation and disintegration argument. If the
analyst knew `Theta`, A-009 says the choice probability would be
`p(y | X,Theta; eta*)`. Because `Theta` is latent, conditioning only on
`(X,Z)` averages that kernel over the regular conditional distribution of
`Theta`. The primary statement stays conditional on both `X` and `Z`, so it
does not require trial-design exogeneity.

The result is deliberately per-observation (or per-trial). It does not assert
a participant-level joint likelihood or a conclusion about a particular LOO,
Bayes-factor, or other model-comparison target.

### 5.2 Step-by-Step Derivation

1. Begin with the population conditional law identified by the observed-data
   distribution:

   ```math
   P(Y = y | X = x, Z = z).
   ```

   This is the population probability of outcome `y` among observations with
   trial features `x` and observed covariates `z`. It does not condition on
   `C`, because `C` is omitted from the analyst's model, or on the realized
   `Theta`, because `Theta` is latent. Consistent estimation requires sampling
   and design conditions outside this lemma.

2. Let `I_y = 1{Y = y}`. The tower property gives

   ```math
   E[I_y \mid X,Z] = E[E[I_y \mid X,Z,\Theta] \mid X,Z].
   ```

   Disintegration with respect to the regular conditional distribution of
   `Theta` then gives, for `(X,Z)`-almost every `(x,z)`,

   ```math
   P(Y = y | X = x, Z = z)
   = \int q_y(x,z,\theta) dF_{\Theta | X,Z}(\theta | x,z),
   ```

   where `q_y` is a version of
   `P(Y = y \mid X = x, Z = z, \Theta = \theta)`.

3. By A-009, `q_y(X,Z,\Theta) = p(y \mid X,\Theta; \eta^*)` almost surely
   under the joint law of `(X,Z,Theta)`. A-010 makes the integrand jointly
   measurable, and boundedness as a choice probability gives integrability.
   Substitution yields

   ```math
   P(Y = y | X = x, Z = z)
   = \int p(y \mid x,\theta; \eta^*)
   dF_{\Theta | X,Z}(\theta | x,z).
   ```

   This is the omitted-context mixture law.

4. Interpret where omitted context enters the mixture. A-002 is not
   load-bearing for the identity. It supplies the project interpretation:

   ```math
   \Theta = h(Z,C,U).
   ```

   If `C` affects `Theta`, part of the spread, skew, or multimodality of
   `F_{\Theta | X,Z}` is context-induced rather than merely idiosyncratic
   noise. The omitted context has disappeared from the analyst's model formula,
   but it has not disappeared from the distribution being averaged over.

5. Derive the fixed/exogenous-design corollary. Under `Theta ⟂ X | Z`,

   ```math
   F_{\Theta | X,Z}(\cdot | x,z) = F_{\Theta | Z}(\cdot | z)
   ```

   for `(X,Z)`-almost every `(x,z)`. A literal fixed conditional design is the
   degenerate case of the same condition. For randomized assignment, a
   sufficient structural condition is `X = g(Z,V)` for measurable `g` with
   `V ⟂ (C,U) | Z`. Since `Theta = h(Z,C,U)`, this implies
   `Theta ⟂ X | Z`. Substitution in the primary mixture law yields the
   corollary.

### 5.3 Conclusion

The analyst's observed response law is not usually a single conditional choice
kernel evaluated at one known latent parameter. It is a mixture of such kernels
over the latent-parameter distribution left after conditioning on observed
data. The primary theorem statement uses `F_{\Theta | X,Z}`. This conclusion is
a per-trial marginal statement; it neither states nor factorizes a
participant-level joint response law. The simplified `F_{\Theta | Z}` statement
is available only as Corollary 1.2 under fixed or exogenous trial design.

## 6. Implications for the Project

1. **The target of trial-wise marginal comparison changes.** For a declared
   trial-wise marginal criterion, a context-omitting model is evaluated against
   `p_0(y | x,z)`, not against the individual-level kernel with `C` represented.
2. **False complexity becomes possible.** If the mixture law has curvature,
   heteroskedasticity, or apparent source-specific effects that are not present
   in the individual-level kernel, a more flexible context-omitting model may
   fit those features better than a simple context-omitting model.
3. **Participant-level targets need their own derivation.** Lemma 1.1 does not
   state a participant-level joint likelihood and does not justify replacing
   that law with a product of trial-level marginals. Participant-level LOO,
   Bayes-factor, or joint-likelihood results must use a separately stated
   target law after the leave-out unit and design process are declared.
4. **The lemma does not prove model-selection failure by itself.** Later
   results must show that the complex model approximates the relevant target
   better under a specified criterion such as KL divergence, expected log score,
   AIC/BIC thresholds, PSIS-LOO ELPD, or held-out log score.
5. **Boundary cases remain central.** If `C` has no effect on `Theta`, if the
   simple model already represents the induced mixture, or if model-selection
   penalties dominate the extra fit, the false-complex model need not win.
6. **The RAID empirical role is diagnostic rather than causal by default.** In
   the empirical track, this lemma motivates comparing context-aware simple
   models against context-omitting complex models. It does not by itself turn
   income or education associations into causal SES effects.

## 7. Known Gaps / Unproven Steps

- The regular conditional distribution and joint kernel measurability are now
  stated explicitly. The finite-outcome identity covers the project's
  binary/multinomial response law but is not stated at the generality needed
  for continuous or otherwise non-finite outcome spaces.
- A-009 and A-010 are proposed, decision-gated assumptions. The proof cannot
  claim that the A-009 conditional-kernel equality follows from the current
  registered wording of A-001 alone.
- This lemma does not prove heteroskedasticity, KL dominance, or finite-sample
  model-selection thresholds.
- Participant-level likelihood, LOO, and Bayes-factor consequences require
  separately stated joint-law and leave-out-unit conditions.
- The proof-critic review is complete with disposition `REVISE`; acceptance is
  pending PI direction on A-009 and A-010 and a renewed proof review.

## 8. Counter-Example Candidates

- Adaptive trial designs: if `X` is assigned based on previous choices that
  reveal information about `Theta`, then `F_{\Theta | X,Z}` may differ from
  `F_{\Theta | Z}`. This defeats the corollary, not the primary Lemma 1.1 when
  its own trial-level conditional-kernel premise A-009 holds.
- Stratified task assignment: if participants with different `Z` or inferred
  latent traits receive different trial distributions, the simplified display
  can misstate the mixture distribution.
- Correctly specified random-effects simple models: the mixture may be
  absorbed without favoring a false complex cognitive mechanism.

## 9. Need for New Assumptions?

Two new proposed, decision-gated assumptions are needed for the revised
primary statement: A-009 states the conditional choice-kernel sufficiency
used in the substitution step, and A-010 states joint kernel measurability.
They are recorded in the assumption register and await PI direction. A-006
remains rejected as a primary assumption. The `F_{\Theta | Z}` display remains
only a corollary under the additional fixed/exogenous-design conditions.
